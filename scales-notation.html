This page is meant as a conceptual guide. It gives you a simple, practical method:

1. What is a Scale?

A scale is a fixed set of notes arranged in order that your song uses ‚Äì like a ‚Äúpalette‚Äù of swars.

  • Indian: Bilawal, Kalyan, Kafi, etc.
  • Western: C major, G minor, etc.
  • A song usually stays inside one main scale or raag.

2. Sa & the Key

Sa is your reference note (the tonic). In Western terms this is the ‚Äúkey‚Äù of the song (C, D, E‚ô≠, etc.).

Once you fix Sa on the harmonium/keyboard, all other notes are counted relative to that Sa.

3. Scale ‚Üí Sargam

After finding Sa and the scale type (major, minor, or raag), every melody note can be named as:

Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni (with komal/tivra where needed).

4. Then Write Notation

Break the song into small phrases (2‚Äì4 beats) and write the corresponding sargam for each beat.

Use dots, lines or bars as per your preferred Bhatkhande style.

How to Find the Scale of a Song

  1. Listen for the ‚Äúhome‚Äù note (Sa).
    This is the note where phrases feel complete and restful.
  2. Try that note on harmonium.
    Sing ‚ÄúSa‚Äù along with different keys until it feels perfect and stable.
  3. Sing the song‚Äôs main line slowly.
    While singing, try to press keys and figure out all notes used.
  4. Check which pattern fits:
    If all notes are shuddha and match the Bilawal pattern, it‚Äôs like a major scale. If some are komal/tivra, it may be a different scale/raag.
  5. Fix your Sa + scale.
    Example: ‚ÄúMy Sa is G, and the song mostly uses shuddha notes ‚Üí G major / Bilawal on G.‚Äù

Writing Bhatkhande-Style Notation Once Scale Is Known

Notation Basics (Sargam)

  • Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni ‚Äì shuddha swars
  • Komal swars ‚Äì re, ga, dha, ni (often written with a line/underline or lowercase)
  • Tivra Ma ‚Äì written as MaÃá or M^ depending on your style
  • Lower octave ‚Äì show with a dot or mark below (e.g., .Sa) if you use that style.
  • Upper octave ‚Äì dot above or another mark (e.g., Sa.).

Beat Grouping

Decide the taal (for example: Teentaal ‚Äì 16 beats). Then group notes by matra:

  • Write 1‚Äì4 notes per beat, depending on speed.
  • Use bars | to mark vibhag, and double bar || to mark sam if you like.

Scales vs Raags ‚Äì Quick Connection

A scale tells you which notes are allowed. A raag tells you not only the notes, but also:

  • Which notes are important (vadi, samvadi).
  • Typical phrases / movements.
  • Ascending vs descending shapes (aaroh‚Äìavroh).
  • Which notes are avoided or used specially.

For many film and light songs, thinking in major / minor scales is enough. For classical and semi-classical, you‚Äôll usually think directly in terms of raag.

Practical Workflow ‚Äì From Song to Notation

  1. Choose your comfortable Sa on harmonium.
  2. Find the scale (major/minor or raag) by checking which notes appear.
  3. Write swars phrase-wise:

    • Find keys, convert to Sa Re Ga‚Ä¶
    • Write into your notebook or SargamWorld Notation Editor.
  4. Add taal structure ‚Äì mark sam, vibhag, and matras.
  5. Review & sing from notation ‚Äì correct any swars that sound off.
